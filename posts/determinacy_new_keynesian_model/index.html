Generated Regressors and the Narrative Approach

September 9, 2023

The economy reacts to monetary policy, but monetary policy also reacts to past, present and expected economic conditions. It is this endogenous (to the economy) behaviour of monetary policy, in it’s aims at price stability and/or full output, that generates difficulties in estimating it’s effects. One would like to separate the changes in monetary policy that are attributed to a systematic response to the economy and those that are not. Those that are not, if credibly separated, can be treated as pseudo-random changes in policy, which facilitate estimation. Let \(S_t\) be the policy variable, \(f(\Omega_t)\) the structural response function given all information up to time \(t\) and \(m_t\) the monetary polich shocks.

\[ S_t = f(\Omega_t) + m_t \]

In fact, this recovered shocks can serve as an instrumental variable, see section 2.1 in Baumeister and Hamilton (2022) .

This identification issue is at the core of the whole Structural Vector Auto-Regression literature, see for example Breitung and Brüggeman (2023) for several identification schemes falling under such approach. The previous narrative is also at the core of more structural (model based) work, such as in the estimation of the New-Keynesian Phillips curve, Mavroeidis, Plagborg-Moller and Stock (2014), and McLeay and Tenreyro (2019).

To this effect, Romer and Romer (2004) and it’s UK brother study of Cloyne and Hürtgen (2016) propose to estimate the following regression by OLS and use the recovered residuals as monetary policy shocks.

\[ \begin{split} \Delta i_m &= \alpha + \beta i_{t-d14} + \sum_{j=-1}^2 \gamma_j \hat{y}^F_{m,j} + \sum_{j=-1}^2\varphi \pi^F_{m,j}\\ & + \sum_{j=-1}^2 \delta_j(\hat{y}^F_{m,j} -\hat{y}^F_{m-1,j}) + \sum_{j=-1}^2 \vartheta(\pi^F_{m,j}-\pi^F_{m-1,j}) + \sum_{j=1}^3\rho_j u_{t-j} + \epsilon_m \end{split} \]
